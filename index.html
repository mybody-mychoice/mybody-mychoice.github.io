Access to Birth Control

If you are located in or have easy access to transportation to an "abortion and birth control - friendly" state, you can get birth control and condoms from pharmacies, hospital, clinics, or Plan Parenthood sites. Depending on your insurance provider, you can get your birth control and abortion procedures for little to no cost. Usually, clinics and hospitals provide free condoms. Also, they provide birth control and abortion services.

However, if you live in a restrictive state, here are some tips:

  • You can ask a trusted acquitance to send you some birth control
  • if you have the financial means, you can travel to non-restrictive state to get the procedure and/or BC meds
  • You can order birth control pills on online, depending on your states law.
  • Some companies provide financial assistance for abortion and other birth control methods. Talk to your company's HR.
  • As of 2022, condoms, spermicide and contraceptive gel, and emergency contraception pills, like Plan B, are available to purchase at drugstores without a prescription
  • As of 2022, Diaphragms, cervical caps, birth control rings, birth control patches, a new contraceptive gel called Phexxi, and birth control pills are available with a prescription.
  • As of 2022, IUDs and sterlization procedures are still legal in all states
  • Some states banned abortion pills. Check you states law for any loopholes

Condoms

A female condom (left) is a thin, flexible plastic pouch that is inserted into the vagina before intercourse to prevent sperm from entering the uterus. This also prevents transmission of STDs.
Effectiveness: 79%

A male condom (right) is a thin sheath that covers the penis that collects and prevent sperm from leaving, thus preventing pregnancy. Those made of latex and polyurethane can help prevent STDs. Lamnskin or other animal membrane condoms do exist for those who are allergic to latex and polyurethane. Though it protects against pregnancy, it does not protect against STD transmission.
Effectiveness: 85%

Contraceptive sponge

A contraceptive sponge is a small sponge that can be inserted into the vagina to cover the cervix (the opening of the uterus). It also contains also contains a spermicide to kill sperm. However, it does not prevent STD transmission.

Effectiveness: 76%

Spermicide

Spermicide is any substance that can kill sperm cells. It typically comes in foam, jelly, cream, suppository, or film. It can be used alone or with a diaphragm or cervical cap. Also, some condoms come with spremicide on them.

Effectiveness: 72%

Diaphragm and Cervical cap

Diaphragm (left) and cervical cap (right) are cups that are inserted inside the vagina to cover the cervix. They may be used with spermicide. They come in diffrent sizes, so talk to your health care provider to find the right size.

Effectiveness: 72%

Oral contraceptives

Oral contraceptives, a.k.a "The Pill" or "Birth Control Pills", are a set of daily medication that uses hormones to prevent pregnancy. The pills typically contain progestin or both progestin and estrogen.

Effectiveness: 91%

Contraceptive patch

A contraceptive patch is a patch that releases hormones through the skin and into the bloodstream to prevent pregnancy.

Effectiveness: 91%

Vaginal Ring

A vaginal ring is a thin, flexible ring that is inserted the ring into the vagina, where it continually releases hormones for three weeks. The ring itself needs to be changed every three weeks.

Effectiveness: 91%

Injectable Birth Control

A injectable birth control is an injection of a hormone that is given by a health care provider once every three months

Effectiveness: > 98%

Implant

A implant is A single, thin rod that a heatlh care provider inserts under the skin of the upper arm. It release hormones that prevent pregnancy up to 4 years.

Effectiveness: > 99%

Intrauterine device

A intrauterine device aka IUD is a small, T-shaped device that is insertsed into the uterus by a health care provider. They can last from 3 to 10 years. There are two types: hormonal IUDs and copper IUDs.

Hormonal IUDS release homrones that prevent pregnancies.

Copper IUD works by the copper in the IUD creates a inflammatory reaction that is toxic to sperm and eggs (ova), thus preventing pregnancy. It can be used as emergency contraceptive in which a provider inserts into the within 120 hours of unprotected intercourse.

Effectiveness: > 99%

Tubal Ligation

A tubal Ligation is a surgical procedure in which the fallopian tubes are surgically disconnected by ways of tying, cauterizing, cutting, or clipping. Depending on the procedure, it can be reversed. Otherwise, it is permanent.

Effectiveness: > 99%

Vasectomy

A vasectomy is a surgical procedure performed on individuals with male genitals. The procedure disconnects the vas deferens from the testicles, preventing sperm from leaving the body. Depending on the procedure, it can be reserved. Otherwise, it is permanent.

Effectiveness: > 99%

Emergency contraceptive pills

Emergency contraceptive pills aka "Plan B" or "Morning After Pills" are hormonal pills that can be taken up to 72 hours after unprotected intercourse. The sooner it is taken, the better chance it has to prevent pregnancy.

Effectiveness: Varies

Fertility Awareness

Fertility awareness-based methods aka "natural rhythm methods" involves monitoring and recording different fertility signals during a menstrual cycle to work out when is the time period to get pregnant. This method has a high risk of pregnancy because the menstrual cycle is not very constant. Extternal factors can affect the menstrual cycle such as stress and diet.

Effectiveness: Varies

Withdrawal

Withdrawal aka "Pulling Out method" is when the penis is pulled of the vagina before ejaculation during intercourse. This method has a high risk of pregnancy because sperm can leak from the penis while pulling out.

Effectiveness: Varies
